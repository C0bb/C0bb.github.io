The Swedish Crayfish Party

In Nordic countries, August is a time for celebration. It's a time for crayfish parties, and gathering with friends and family to enjoy those warm summer nights. Better known as a kräftskiva in Sweden, this tradition dates back to the early 1900s when crayfish restrictions were introduced. That tradition continues today, and has spread to Finland thanks to its Swedish-speaking population.


The Origin of the Crayfish Party

The people of Sweden have been eating crayfish since the 1500s, but for a long while, only aristocracy could afford such delicacies. In its early days, crayfish was used to make ragout, sausage, puddings and patties.

By the 1800s, people were eating crayfish just as they are today. The August tradition of a crayfish feast spread to the middle classes, and would become a national delicacy by the 1900s.

Thanks to crayfish imports from Turkey and other parts of the world, prices for this delicacy fell and became more affordable for all sectors of society. Crayfish parties, or feasts, are still held in August to mark the end of the summer season. This is a time when Swedish people gather together to eat, drink and be merry.

Due to concerns of overfishing, restrictions were put in place for crayfishing in the early 1900s, and the August celebrations were born.

Today, crayfish can be imported all year round, but many Swedes still prefer to celebrate this seasonal tradition the old fashioned way. Although still expensive, Swedish crayfish are cooked in a brine with lots of crown dill.


The Celebration

Traditionally, crayfish parties are held outdoors. But bad weather and pesky mosquitos often drive the celebration indoors. Paper lanterns, comical paper hats, paper tablecloths and bibs are traditional décor items you'll find in a typical crayfish party. Often times, you'll see paper lanterns depicting the Man in the Moon.

Fun Fact: The tradition of wearing paper hats began as a way to poke fun at the upper class and their posh hats.

No crayfish party is complete without a rowdy atmosphere. Partygoers sing traditional drinking songs and enjoy an evening of noisy eating. You'll find akvavit, snaps and beer served at most parties.


How the Crayfish is Prepared

Traditionally, the crayfish are boiled in salty water and then seasoned with fresh dill. Crown dill that is freshly picked just after flowering is preferred. Typically, the crayfish is served cold and beaten with your hands.

It's customary to suck the juice out of the crayfish before you shell it.

Along with the crayfish, sustromming, mushroom pies, bread, salads, Vasterbotten cheese and a variety of other dishes are served.

How much crayfish do you need? Plan to serve 1lb per person. Many Swedes serve a mixture of crayfish and prawns because crayfish can be so expensive. And those who are lucky enough to fish for their own often find it difficult to catch enough to serve a party.

Crayfish parties in Sweden are a lot like Christmas parties. The whole family gets together to enjoy a night of eating, drinking and merriment.

It's an old tradition; the perfect excuse to party and eat unhealthy amounts of crustaceans.
